## Why this catalog exists, and the bar it clears

The arena's differentiator is **trust**, not graphics — Henry Onyx's one honest standard applied to play. Two design constraints flow from that, and every game below is built to satisfy both:

1. **Skill must demonstrably predominate over chance.** This matters first as a *fairness* property (a better player should win more often) and second, and decisively, as a *legal-classification* property: the eventual real-money layer (`PHASED-PLAN.md`, Pass 3+) is defensible only where the law recognises a *game of skill*, and the "skill predominance" test is the line many jurisdictions draw. We therefore engineer chance **down** and skill **up** in every title — and we lead with a game that has *zero* chance at all.
2. **The free-play foundation needs no money and no legal gate.** Every game here is fully playable for **free practice and ranked-for-pride PvP** with no stake and no prize. In that mode there is no *consideration* and no *prize* — two of the three elements of gambling are absent — so the free arena ships on registration while counsel reviews the money layer. See `LEGAL-GATE-MAP.md`.

A note on how we keep chance fair where it exists: any randomness a game *does* use (a prize order, a question draw) is generated by the **provably-fair commit–reveal engine** specified in `ARCHITECTURE.md §6`, and it is **symmetric** — both players face the *same* randomness. Symmetric, verifiable randomness is not a chance *advantage* to either player; it is shared, transparent terrain that skill is exercised over. This is the heart of the "provably fair" promise.

### Originality

Every title is an **original work** — original mechanics, original naming, original board/card/question design. None is a re-skin of Whot, Ludo, UNO, a branded trivia format, Hex, GOPS/Goofspiel, or any existing commercial or traditional game. Where a title sits in a known *family* of games (connection games; simultaneous-selection games), that lineage is noted honestly below so counsel can assess novelty, and the specific differentiating mechanics are called out. The rules documents that ship with the build (`docs/gaming/onyx-*-rules.md`) are the counsel-reviewed artifacts (the `L7` inputs); this catalog is the design intent behind them.

### The skill-vs-chance ladder

| Game | Genre | Chance element | How chance is neutralised | `skillWeight` (design target) | Provably-fair RNG used? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Onyx Lines** | Abstract strategy / connection board | **None** | N/A — perfect information, deterministic | **1.00** | No (none needed — and that is the point) |
| **Onyx Cards** | Simultaneous-selection card duel | Prize-value order | **Symmetric** (identical for both) + **commit–reveal** seed | **0.92** | Yes |
| **Onyx Quiz** | Head-to-head knowledge duel | Which questions are drawn | **Symmetric** (same questions, same timer) + commit–reveal seed + **category draft** skill layer | **0.85** | Yes |

> `skillWeight` is a design-stated estimate of skill predominance, **not** a legal determination. The binding skill-vs-chance classification per market is the counsel opinion (`L7`); these numbers feed that review and the responsible-play model, nothing more.

The ladder is deliberate. **Onyx Lines is the flagship**: a game with no randomness whatsoever needs no RNG trust, makes the cleanest possible "game of skill" argument, and is the simplest to build flawlessly. Onyx Cards and Onyx Quiz then demonstrate the provably-fair engine on the two places randomness legitimately appears — and even there, the randomness is shared, not dealt to one side.

## 1. Onyx Lines — the flagship (zero chance, perfect information)

### Concept
A two-player **connection-strategy** game on a custom rhombic hex board. Each player owns two opposite edges and races to build an unbroken chain of their own stones linking those edges. There is **no randomness, no hidden information, and no dice or cards** — the entire game is visible to both players at all times, and the outcome is determined solely by the quality of the moves. It belongs to the *connection-game family* (the lineage that includes Hex and Havannah); its originality is in the **veined board topology**, the **swap-balance rule**, and the optional **bridge-scoring** finish described below.

### Board
- A rhombus of **hexagonal cells**, 11×11 for ranked play (the size is a tuning parameter, not fixed in stone).
- Two opposite edges are **Onyx edges** (Player A's goal edges); the other two are **Alabaster edges** (Player B's).
- A short diagonal run of **pre-blocked "vein" cells** crosses the board off-centre. The vein is fixed and identical every match (it is part of the board, not random) — it creates asymmetric terrain that rewards reading the board over rote opening lines, and is the principal originality marker versus a plain connection board.

### Turn structure
1. Players alternate. On your turn you place **one stone of your colour on any one empty cell**. That is the whole move. (Optional placed stones are never moved or removed thereafter — except by the one special action below.)
2. **First-move balance (swap rule).** After Player A's first stone, Player B may either play normally **or** "swap" — claim A's opening stone as their own and pass the turn back. This is the standard, elegant fix for the first-mover advantage inherent to connection games; it makes the opening a *skill* decision (A must open neither too strong nor too weak) rather than a solved advantage.
3. **Fracture (one per player per match).** Once per match, instead of placing, a player may **fracture**: convert a single opponent stone that is *adjacent to two or more of the fracturing player's own stones* into a neutral blocker. This adds a bounded, fully-visible tactical resource without introducing any randomness. (Fracture is the second originality marker; it is omitted in the "pure" ranked variant for players who want a Hex-clean game — selectable per lobby.)

### How a player wins (and why skill alone decides)
- **Win condition:** the first player to complete an unbroken, edge-to-edge chain of their own stones connecting their two edges wins immediately. Connection is computed server-side with a union-find over the move log.
- In the pure variant (no fracture), the board topology guarantees that **exactly one player can connect** and **draws are impossible** — a mathematically clean outcome that depends on nothing but play. With the fracture variant, a rare mutual-block can occur; it resolves to the player with the **longer single connected chain** (bridge-scoring), and failing that, to a single deterministic tiebreak move-count rule — never to chance.
- **Skill determination:** the game has **no chance input of any kind**. Both players see the entire board, move under identical rules, and the swap rule removes the structural first-move edge. The winner is the player who reads threats, builds double-connections ("bridges") that cannot be cut, and forces the opponent into a losing tempo. This is the strongest skill claim in the catalog: there is simply no luck to attribute a loss to.

### Why it is the right flagship to build first
- **Trivial, auditable state machine:** place stone → validate cell empty → append move → recompute connectivity → maybe terminal. Server-authoritative truth is straightforward and cheap.
- **No RNG, so no RNG trust problem:** Onyx Lines needs neither the commit–reveal engine nor any "trust us, the deal was fair" explanation. Its fairness is *self-evident from the move log* — anyone can replay it.
- **Pure turn-based:** no clocks required for correctness (an optional move-timer is a UX/anti-stall layer, not a rule), so it works over the simplest transport (`ARCHITECTURE.md §4`) and is the ideal Pass-1 launch title.

## 2. Onyx Cards — simultaneous-selection duel (symmetric, minimal chance)

### Concept
A two-player card duel in which **both players hold the identical hand** and compete, round by round, to win a public sequence of prizes by committing the right card at the right moment. Because the hands are identical and the prize sequence is the same for both, the only variable is **decision quality** — the game is "the same problem, solved by two minds." It belongs to the *simultaneous-selection family* (the lineage that includes Goofspiel/GOPS); its originality is in the **facet bonus**, the **vein carry** tie rule, and the **shadow bid** described below, and in sourcing its one random element from the **provably-fair engine**.

### Components
- Each player holds an **identical hand** of value cards, **1 through 10**, in their own colour. (Mirror-dealt: there is no luck of the draw — you and your opponent start with exactly the same resources.)
- A **prize track** of 10 prize cards, each worth a published point value, laid out in an order fixed at match start. The *order* is the game's single chance element — and it is **identical for both players** and **provably fair** (below).
- Each prize card also carries a **facet** (one of three colours). Some of your value cards share a facet.

### The one random element, made fair
The prize-track order is produced by the **commit–reveal RNG** in `ARCHITECTURE.md §6`: before the match the server publishes `commit = hash(serverSeed)`; each player contributes a `clientSeed`; the prize order is derived deterministically from `serverSeed ⊕ clientSeeds`; after the match the server reveals `serverSeed`, and either player (or a third party) can recompute the order and confirm it was neither rigged nor changed mid-game. Crucially, **both players play against the same order**, so the randomness confers no edge — it is shared terrain, fully known to both at the first move.

### Turn structure
1. Ten rounds. Each round both players **secretly commit one card** from their remaining hand (server holds both commits; neither sees the other's until both are in).
2. **Reveal.** Both cards turn over. The **higher value wins the round's prize** and adds its points to that player's score. The two committed cards are then spent (removed from hand) — managing a finite, shared resource across the known prize sequence is the core skill.
3. **Facet bonus (originality):** if the winning card's facet matches the prize's facet, the winner scores a **bonus**. This rewards planning *which* card to spend, not merely *how high*.
4. **Vein carry (originality):** if both players commit equal values, the prize is **not awarded** — it "carries" onto the next round's prize, so the next round is worth more. This turns ties into strategic stakes-raising rather than dead rounds.
5. **Shadow bid (one per match, originality):** once per match a player may flag their committed card as a **shadow bid**, secretly doubling its value for that round — but if they *lose* the round anyway, the doubled card is revealed to have been a shadow bid, leaking information about their remaining hand. A bounded, high-skill bluff lever.

### How a player wins (and why skill predominates)
- **Win condition:** highest total prize points after 10 rounds. A points tie is broken by who **won the higher-value individual prize**, then by fewest cards spent on losses — deterministic, never random.
- **Skill determination:** the players begin with **identical hands** and **identical, known information** about the prize sequence (it is public from move one). No card is drawn during play; nothing is hidden except the current round's simultaneous commit, which both players face symmetrically. The outcome is therefore a function of *sequencing, tempo, prediction, and bluff management* — i.e., skill. The lone stochastic element (prize order) is symmetric and provably fair, so it cannot be the cause of one player winning over the other. Over a best-of-N match the residual variance shrinks further.

## 3. Onyx Quiz — head-to-head knowledge duel (symmetric questions + a strategy layer)

### Concept
A two-player knowledge duel where both players answer the **same questions** under the **same timer**, and a **category draft** lets each player steer the contest toward their strengths and away from the opponent's. Knowledge and recall speed are the core skill; the draft adds a strategic meta-layer so the game is more than raw trivia luck. Questions come from a **Henry-Onyx-owned** question bank — no third-party question sets, no licensed formats.

### Components
- A **category board** of public categories for the match (e.g., a balanced spread across general knowledge domains; the taxonomy is Henry-Onyx-authored).
- A **question bank** owned by Henry Onyx, tagged by category and difficulty, stored server-side and never shipped to the client ahead of time (anti-cheat: the client never holds unanswered questions).

### Turn structure
1. **Category draft (the skill meta-layer, originality):** players alternately **ban** and then **pick** categories from the board — you remove a category your opponent might be strong in, and select one you are strong in. The drafted categories define the round set. This is pure strategy, with perfect information, and it is where a knowledgeable player shapes the match in their favour before a single question is asked.
2. **Rounds:** for each drafted category, both players are served the **same 5 questions** in the **same order** under a **shared countdown**. Questions are selected from the bank by the **provably-fair commit–reveal seed** (so neither player nor the house can hand-pick easy/hard questions, and the selection is verifiable afterwards) — and, again, the selection is **identical for both players**.
3. **Answer:** both answer independently; the server scores **correctness first**, then uses **cumulative response time as the tiebreak**. The client never receives the answer key; grading is entirely server-side.

### How a player wins (and why skill predominates)
- **Win condition:** most correct answers across all rounds; ties broken by total response time, then by a single sudden-death question drawn from a neutral category. Deterministic resolution.
- **Skill determination:** both players face **identical questions** under an **identical timer**, so no player is advantaged by an easier draw — the "did I happen to get a question I knew" luck is removed by symmetry. The category draft is a perfect-information strategy contest. The result reflects **knowledge breadth, recall speed, and drafting skill**. The only stochastic element (which questions within a category) is symmetric and provably fair.
- **Integrity note:** because knowledge games invite answer-lookup cheating, Onyx Quiz leans hardest on the anti-cheat layer (`ARCHITECTURE.md §7`): short shared timers that make external lookup impractical, server-side grading with no client-side key, response-time anomaly detection, and (in the money layer) the fair-play audit. The free-play tier tolerates more risk because nothing is at stake; the stakes tier raises the bar.

## Catalog data model (design intent)

Each game is a typed `GameDefinition` in the arena package's catalog (see `ARCHITECTURE.md §5` for the package and `§3` for the schema). The shape the build will implement:

```ts
export type GameId = "onyx-lines" | "onyx-cards" | "onyx-quiz";

export type GameDefinition = {
  id: GameId;
  nameKey: string;            // i18n key into surface:gaming — never a hardcoded display string
  descriptionKey: string;
  minPlayers: 2;
  maxPlayers: number;         // 2 for all launch titles (head-to-head)
  /** Design-stated skill predominance; feeds the L7 review + responsible-play model. NOT a legal ruling. */
  skillWeight: number;
  /** Whether this game consumes the provably-fair RNG (false for Onyx Lines). */
  usesRandomness: boolean;
  /** Path to the counsel-reviewed rules doc — the L7 input artifact. */
  rulesDocPath: string;
  /** Pure server-side validator: is this proposed move legal given current state? */
  validateMove: GameMoveValidator;
  /** Deterministic initial state from the provably-fair seed + seated players. */
  initialState: (seed: string, players: PlayerSeat[]) => GameState;
  /** Terminal detection + winner resolution — server-side only. */
  resolveOutcome: (state: GameState) => GameOutcome;
};
```

> Naming note: the catalog uses `onyx-lines` as the flagship id. The original V3-65 prompt named the third title `onyx-track` (a board *race* game). This design **supersedes** that with `onyx-lines` (a perfect-information connection game) for a stronger zero-chance skill claim; a race game with movement randomness would *weaken* the skill classification we are optimising for. `PHASED-PLAN.md` records this as an intentional refinement of V3-65's catalog. `onyx-cards` and `onyx-quiz` are carried forward and refined for symmetry.

### Enablement gating (per game, per market)
A game appears in the **enabled** catalog only when:
- **Free-play tier:** its rules doc exists and is internally reviewed for content-appropriateness (no gambling clearance required — there is no stake or prize).
- **Stakes tier (gated):** additionally, a **counsel-countersigned `L7`** classifies it as a game of skill *in that market*, an `L15` AML review is on file (where required), and a per-market enablement row asserts it. No game is staked in any market lacking all three. See `LEGAL-GATE-MAP.md`.

## What is deliberately *not* in the launch catalog

- **Real-time / reflex games** (anything where outcome depends on reaction speed or continuous input) — deferred to a later real-time phase (`PHASED-PLAN.md`). Turn-based first: easier to build flawlessly, fairer, far cheaper to make server-authoritative, and the variance is fully under design control.
- **Multiplayer (3+) tables** — head-to-head first. Multi-seat introduces collusion surface and payout complexity best added after the 2-player base is proven.
- **High-variance card/dice games** — anything where a random draw can hand a round to a weaker player works against the skill-predominance bar and is excluded by design.
- **Any third-party or traditional game** — Whot, Ludo, UNO, branded trivia, chess/draughts variants, Hex/GOPS as-is. Original works only.
